The Agent Stack Bet

AI assistants keep crashing in the real world — not because they're dumb, but because nobody built the right foundation underneath them. Here's what's missing and how to fix it.

Plain English summary — Imagine hiring a new employee, but you never give them their own login, only let them see one filing cabinet at a time, tell them their memory resets every 4 hours, and make them rebuild their own desk every morning. That's what most AI agents deal with today. This article argues there are four specific things to fix — and teams that don't fix them in the next year will get stuck.

What even is an "agent"?

An AI agent is a program that doesn't just answer questions — it takes actions. It can browse the web, read your emails, update a spreadsheet, file a support ticket, or call an API. You give it a goal and it figures out the steps.

Companies are now trying to use agents for serious business tasks: process hundreds of invoices, flag contracts that look risky, monitor customer churn. These tasks take hours, touch multiple systems, and can't afford to make silent mistakes.

The problem? Most of the technology underneath agents was built for chatbots — quick questions and answers. When you ask an agent to do a 3-day task, the foundations start cracking.

The four fixes, explained simply

Engineer and author Addy Osmani calls these the "four bets." Each one is a shift from something that works in a 10-minute demo to something that works when real money and real data are on the line.

Fix 1 · Give it its own identity Bet #1
Analogy: Imagine five employees all sharing one username and password. When something goes wrong — a file is deleted, a wire transfer is approved — you have no idea who did it. You can't fire the person or revoke their access, because there's no "person" to point to.

Today, most agents borrow a login from a human employee or share a single "service account" with other systems. The company's security tools can't tell the agent apart from a person, or from other agents running alongside it.

The fix is to give each agent its own unforgeable digital ID — like a badge that only it can carry. When the agent does something, that action is stamped with its specific identity. You can see exactly what it accessed, and if you need to stop it, you just revoke its badge.

  • Every action the agent takes gets a clear "who did this" label in the audit log
  • You can cut an agent's access instantly, without affecting any humans or other agents
  • Security teams can actually answer "what did our agents do last week?"
Fix 2 · Let it see all the data at once Bet #2
Analogy: Imagine a detective who can only look at one filing cabinet at a time — they can read police reports OR financial records OR witness statements, but never all three together. Their conclusions will always be incomplete.

Most agents today are like that detective. They can connect to your invoice database, or your vendor contract system, or your payment history — but not all three simultaneously. That means they can't make connections between systems, which is exactly where the interesting insights live.

The fix is a shared layer that connects all your business systems so the agent can reason across all of them at once. It can spot that the invoice amount doesn't match the contract price because it can see both at the same time.

  • Without this, agents are just slightly smarter search boxes for one system at a time
  • With this, an agent can spot a vendor overcharging because it sees the contract and the invoice together
  • This is the hardest fix but unlocks the most value — cross-system reasoning is where AI earns its keep
Fix 3 · Give it a real memory Bet #3
Analogy: Imagine a contractor who, every time they leave for lunch, completely forgets everything they were working on. You'd have to brief them from scratch every afternoon. No real project would ever get finished.

Today, most agents have a "session" — a window of time during which they remember what they're doing. When the session ends (because of a timeout, a crash, or just a long lunch break), everything is gone. For a task that takes 3 days, this is a dealbreaker.

The fix is persistent memory with checkpoints — like a save file in a video game. The agent saves its progress as it goes. If it gets interrupted, it picks up exactly where it left off, even days later, even if the underlying software has been updated.

  • Real business tasks take days: approving a purchase order, running a compliance check, tracking a delivery
  • The agent also needs to know when to pause and ask a human for permission, rather than guessing
  • "Persistence with guardrails" — it remembers everything, but it doesn't act alone on things it shouldn't
Fix 4 · Stop rebuilding the same plumbing Bet #4
Analogy: Every time you build a new house, you shouldn't be inventing electricity from scratch. Wiring, pipes, and broadband are solved — you plug into them. But most AI teams are currently laying their own cables every single time.

Right now, smart engineering teams spend months building the same basic things over and over: how to retry a failed request, how to log what the agent did, how to track down a bug, how to test that the agent is working correctly. Every team reinvents these wheels independently.

The fix is to use a platform that has already solved all of that — one that handles the boring-but-hard reliability problems so your team can focus on the parts only your business can build: the unique logic, the domain knowledge, the competitive advantage.

  • Engineering time spent on retry logic is engineering time not spent on your actual product
  • A good platform lets you develop on your laptop with the same tools you'll use in production — no surprise behaviour when you deploy
  • Open platforms matter so you're not locked into one vendor forever

Why it's urgent

You might wonder: can't teams just fix these things slowly over time? The article's argument is no — and it's an economic argument, not a technical one.

Every month you spend building your own identity system, your own memory layer, your own logging tools, is a month you're not spending on the thing only your company can build. Your competitors who pick a good foundation early will spend that time building better products instead.

Osmani puts the window at about 12 months. After that, teams that went the custom-build route will be stuck maintaining their custom infrastructure rather than shipping new capabilities on top of it.

The hidden cost: accumulated risk

There's one more idea in the article worth understanding: governance debt. This is the invisible risk that builds up when you run agents without proper infrastructure.

It's not like a bug that crashes your app. It's quieter than that. Over months, you accumulate: actions you can't trace, credentials you can't revoke, work that disappears without warning, and systems so custom-built that one person leaving means nobody understands them anymore.

Plain English glossary
AI Agent
A program that takes actions (clicking, writing, calling APIs) on your behalf — not just answering questions.
Governance Debt
The invisible risk that quietly builds up when you skip proper infrastructure — like leaving your front door unlocked a little every day.
Session
The window of time during which an agent is "awake." When a session ends, the agent forgets everything unless it has persistent memory.
Service Account
A shared login used by programs instead of humans. When multiple agents share one, you lose track of who did what.
Audit Trail
A log of every action taken — who did it, when, and what changed. Essential for knowing what went wrong (or right).
Checkpoint
A saved snapshot of where the agent is in a task — like a save file. Lets it resume after a crash or restart.
Orchestration
The system that coordinates an agent's steps, retries failures, and tracks progress. The "project manager" for the agent.
Universal Context
When an agent can see all your business systems at once — invoices, contracts, emails — instead of one at a time.
